Dr Norman Borlaug

The man who saved abillion lives

Here's a timeline of Dr. Borlaug's life

  • 1914 - Born in Cresco, Iowa
  • 1933 - Leaves his family's farm to attend the Univeristy of Minenesota , thanks to a Depression era program known as the "National Youth Administration"
  • 1935 - Has to stop school and save up more money. Works in the Civilian Conservation Corps, helping starvation Americans. "I saw how food changed them", he said. "All of this left scars on me."
  • 1937 - Finishes University and takes a job in the US Forestry Service
  • 1938 Marries wife of 69 years Magaret Gibson. Gets laid off due to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakeman, he returns to school study under Stakman, who teaches him abour breeding pest-resistent plants.
  • 1941 - Tries to enroll in the military after the pearl Habor attack. but is rejected. Instead, the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue
  • 1942 - Receives a PHD in Genetics and Plant Pathology
  • 1944 - Rejects a 100% salary increases from Dupant, leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years , his team breeds 6,000 different strains of disease resistant wheat - including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.
  • 1945 - Discovers a way to grown whear twice each season, doubling wheat yields.
  • 1953 - crosses a short, sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yielding American breed, creating a strain tht responds well to fertilizer . It goes on to provide 95% of Mexico wheat.
  • 1962 - Visits Delhi and brings his high-yielding stains of wheat to the Indian subcontinent in time to help mitigate mass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population
  • 1970 - receives the Nobel Prize
  • 1983- helps seven African countries dramatically increase their maize and soghum yields
  • 1984 - becomes a distinguished professor at Texas A&M Univeristy
  • 2005 - sates "we will have to double the world food supply by 2050." Argues that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet the deman, as we run out of arable land. Says that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because "we've been genetically modifying plansts and animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people were selecting the best breeds."
  • 2009 - dies at the age of 95.
